How to Get to Caleta Tortel

A guide for those who choose to travel to the end of the road

Reaching Tortel is not just about moving across a map. It means heading south until the landscape changes its rhythm: the asphalt disappears, cell service becomes intermittent, and the journey begins to feel more present.

Many people write to us asking how to get here. The answer is simple: via the Carretera Austral. However, the journey has its own timing, its pauses, and a few decisions that are best made before setting off.

Here, we break down the main ways to reach Tortel and what you need to know for each option.

Arriving by Car: Traveling the Carretera Austral at Your Own Pace

The most common way to get here is by car, especially for those who are already traveling along the Carretera Austral.

From Coyhaique, it is between an eight to nine-hour drive (452 km). From Cochrane, the last urban point before Tortel, the journey takes about three hours (126 km).

The road combines paved sections with long stretches of gravel. It is not a difficult route, but it does require full attention, especially if it has rained or if there is frost. As you move further south, the landscape becomes more open, more humid, and more silent.

The turnoff to Tortel, Route X-904, appears after the junction to Puerto Yungay. From there, it is a 22-kilometer gravel road that descends toward the fjord.

A few tips we always share:

  • Fill up your tank in Cochrane, though Tortel also has a Copec gas station.
  • Check the road conditions before setting off.
  • Allow more time than estimated if you want to travel without rushing.

On this stretch, the drive stops being just transit and truly becomes part of the experience.

Arriving by Bus: Another Way to Enter the Territory

It is also possible to arrive by bus, although it requires a little more planning.

There are no direct routes from Coyhaique. Buses depart from Cochrane, but frequencies are not always daily and vary depending on the season. There are more options in the summer; fewer in the winter.

Those who travel this way usually do so with a different mindset: the rhythm is set by the route, not by one's own itinerary. It is a more leisurely way to arrive, allowing you to observe the landscape more closely.

Our advice is always the same:

  • Check updated bus schedules well in advance.
  • Book your tickets ahead during the high season.
  • Plan for more than one day per location to truly explore the territory with time.

Can You Arrive by Ferry from Puerto Natales?

There is also the possibility of arriving by sailing through the southern channels. It is a less common route, but arguably one of the most memorable.

A ferry operated by the company TABSA runs from Puerto Natales, connecting with the southern part of the Aysén Region. The navigation takes around 41 to 46 hours, traveling through fjords, channels, glaciers, and completely isolated areas.

The journey is not always direct to Tortel: the main route connects Puerto Natales with Puerto Yungay, making intermediate stops in Puerto Edén and Caleta Tortel for passengers to board and disembark.

If you are traveling on foot, you can disembark directly in Tortel. If you are traveling with a vehicle, the most common practice is to disembark in Puerto Yungay and then continue by land to the town.

It is important to keep in mind that:

  • The service operates weekly or on a variable schedule depending on the season.
  • It requires booking well in advance.
  • Schedules are subject to change due to weather conditions.

Before You Travel: What to Keep in Mind

Tortel is an isolated place, and that is a key part of its character. Getting here requires organization, but also a willingness to adapt to the territory.

A few useful recommendations:

  • Plan your fuel stops and travel times carefully.
  • Keep in mind that cell service is limited along most of the route.
  • Check weather conditions before setting off.
  • Travel with extra time to spare, especially during the winter.
  • Book your accommodation in advance during the high season.

Arriving Is Part of the Journey

There is a moment, on the final stretch, when the road begins to descend and the fjord comes into view. From that point on, Tortel is no longer just a destination: it is a place you begin to understand through the very experience of having arrived.
